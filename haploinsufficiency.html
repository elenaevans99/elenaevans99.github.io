When half isn't enough

Elena Evans
· Haploinsufficiency · Drug discovery · Published in Drug Discovery Today, 2024

When Dr. Zheng asked me if I was interested in writing a review on treatment approaches to haploinsufficiency, I wasn't even sure I knew what that was.

Sure, I had been studying OI, a genetic disorder that causes brittle bones, and I had taken a genetics class in college. But that did not mean I knew everything about genetics. One of the things I've struggled with, especially as a graduate student, is accepting that I can't know everything.

I was honored that Dr. Zheng asked. I was also nervous. I didn't have a lot of training in drug development, and I was not a genetics expert. But I knew how to search through published papers and find examples. Dr. Zheng had deep expertise in therapeutic development, and we worked with a genetics expert to make sure our reasoning was sound.

Haploinsufficiency means ½ is not sufficient. It's one of those words that, once you understand it, you start seeing everywhere.

Everyone gets two copies of each chromosome, and therefore two copies of each gene. Sometimes you can have a defect in one copy that doesn't disrupt the overall function; there's still one good copy working, so no big deal.

But in some cases, the damage is too much. The body can't maintain normal operations without two fully functional copies of the gene. That's haploinsufficiency.

It's considered a dominant loss-of-function condition. In other words: the defect only needs to affect one copy of the gene to cause problems (dominant), and it decreases the amount of functional protein being made (loss-of-function).

So how do you fix it? It depends on the gene — but the general strategies are:

  • Increase the amount of RNA being made from the remaining healthy gene copy
  • Increase the amount of functional protein being produced
  • Protect the protein that is being made from being broken down too quickly

The goal for all of these approaches is essentially the same: use the copy of the gene that doesn't have a defect to rescue the function lost by the damaged copy.

This review helped me understand not just haploinsufficiency but also something about how science gets done. You don't have to be an expert in everything. You have to be willing to learn, ask the right people the right questions, and be honest about what you don't know.
