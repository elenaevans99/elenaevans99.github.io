About me

Science that starts and ends with people

I'm a PhD student in Human Genetics at Vanderbilt University, where I work in the Below Lab at the Vanderbilt Genetics Institute. My current research focuses on using computational approaches to investigate the genetic and phenotypic landscape of conditions including celiac disease, osteoarthritis, and hypophosphatasia.

Before Vanderbilt, I spent two years as a Post-Baccalaureate IRTA Fellow at the NIH. , in the Marini Lab (studying osteogenesis imperfecta in human primary cells and mouse models) of the NICHD and the NCATS Therapeutic Development Branch led by Wei Zheng (working with iPSCs and liver organoids to understand Alagille syndrome). The NIH is a special place. It's where I met the people whose cells and mouse models I worked on, where I learned about drug repurposing and high-throughput screening.

As a patient-scientist, I'm always looking for ways that we can improve healthcare, whether that's through scientific discovery or public policy.

If I lost you at "hypophosphatasia," I get it! By the time I stepped foot in a lab, I'd already spent quite a bit of time as a patient. Science has a LOT of terms and acronyms. One of my goals is to communicate my research without the jargon, whether that's through writing, diagrams, or zines (tiny magazines).

Computational Genetics · Complex Disease

Genetic architecture of celiac disease beyond HLA

Investigating genetic associations in celiac disease among carriers of permissive HLA-DQ haplotypes, using computational approaches to characterize both the genetic and phenotypic landscape of this complex condition.

GWASHLA haplotypesCeliac diseaseComputational

Why do some people with celiac risk genes stay healthy?

Celiac disease is strongly linked to certain immune gene variants — but many people who carry those variants never develop the condition. We're asking: what other genetic factors tip the balance? Understanding this could point toward new ways to predict, prevent, or treat celiac disease.

Presented at Digestive Disease Week 2026 and the American Society of Human Genetics Meeting 2025.

Rare Disease · Functional Genetics

Novel variant characterization in osteogenesis imperfecta

Characterized a novel splice mutation in type I collagen (COL1A2) and a variant of unknown significance (VUS) in calcium channel protein (TRPV4), both carried by a patient with osteogenesis imperfecta.

OICOL1A2TRPV4Variant discovery

A patient with two rare mutations that might affect bone health — what does that mean?

Osteogenesis imperfecta (OI) is a condition where bones break more easily than normal, usually caused by changes in collagen genes. We studied a patient who had changes in two different genes at once — a rare combination — and worked out how those mutations affected cellular function. Published in JBMR Plus, 2025.

iPSC Biology · Alagille Syndrome

iPSC lines and CRISPR correction for Alagille syndrome

Generated and characterized iPSC lines from two patients with Alagille syndrome carrying heterozygous JAG1 mutations, and developed CRISPR/Cas9-corrected isogenic lines for therapeutic screening and disease modeling.

iPSCCRISPRJAG1Alagille syndrome

Building a "disease in a dish" to test treatments for Alagille syndrome

Alagille syndrome is a rare genetic condition that affects the liver and other organs. Using cells donated by patients, we grew them into a type of stem cell that can become almost any cell in the body — then used genetic editing tools to "fix" the mutation. These corrected cells let us test potential treatments in a system has the overall genetics of the actual patient. Published in Stem Cell Research, 2025.

Read the full essay → These cell lines are shared openly so other researchers can use them too.

Review · Rare Disease Therapeutics

Therapeutic approaches to haploinsufficiency diseases

A review of current and emerging strategies to restore protein levels in diseases caused by haploinsufficiency — where one functional gene copy is insufficient for normal biology.

HaploinsufficiencyDrug discoveryRare diseaseReview

When one isn't enough — how might we fix that?

Some conditions happen because a person only has one functional copy of a gene instead of two, and that single copy can't do the job alone. We reviewed all different scientific strategies being explored to restore normal protein levels in these situations — from gene therapy to small molecules. Published in Drug Discovery Today, 2024.

Read the full essay → This review helps researchers see the full therapeutic landscape and avoid reinventing the wheel.
