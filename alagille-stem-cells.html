Lay summary · iPSC biology

Blood cells to investigate a rare liver disease

Elena Evans
· Alagille syndrome · iPSC & CRISPR · Published in Stem Cell Research, 2025

One of the ways that science is trying to use non-animal models — and really match the human body whenever possible — is to use stem cells.

For this project, cells from two patients' blood were turned into inducible pluripotent stem cells, or iPSCs. This just means they can be grown into different cell types, and even organoids.

What's an organoid?

Organoids are pretty much what they sound like — tiny versions of organs grown in a plastic dish.

Liver organoids don't actually grow in the shape of little pink livers, like I pictured when Grey's Anatomy mentioned it. They're usually spherical blobs. But they mimic the biological architecture of a real liver remarkably well.

Before you can actually do experiments on organoids — like testing treatments — you have to make sure they're actually behaving like they should. Cells have a knack for doing what they shouldn't. And because these cells have so much potential, they can become a lot of things. So we have to check.

There were two lines of cells: one from a little girl and one from a little boy. Both had a variant in the gene JAG1, which caused Alagille syndrome — an autosomal dominant disorder, meaning having a defect in just one copy of the gene is enough to cause disease. It is very dangerous for the liver but can also affect bones in the spine or face.

We checked that these cell lines had all the markers that indicated they were inducible and pluripotent. We checked that they had the right number of chromosomes. We made sure they hadn't become contaminated. Then we deposited them into a shared resource so other researchers could use them too.

The next thing I worked on was using CRISPR-Cas9 to correct the defect in each cell line, then confirm they were still behaving as expected. CRISPR-Cas9 is essentially a molecular editing tool that can change genetic code — but it's not always perfect, so verification is a critical step.

These corrected, isogenic cell lines give researchers a powerful tool: you can compare the diseased version of a cell directly with the corrected version, with everything else held equal. That's how you start to test whether a potential treatment actually works — and why building and validating these lines carefully matters so much.
